# Amber: First-Person Narration Voice

Load this file when writing any chapter from Amber's POV (first person). This is the dominant mode of the story. For her *spoken dialogue*, load `char-amber-dialogue.md`.

---

## The Voice in One Paragraph

Amber is a college-age Pokemon fan who wakes up in a child's body. Her narration carries analytical capability — game knowledge, pattern recognition, social awareness beyond her apparent age — but her personality was never particularly mature to begin with. She was already excitable, reference-heavy, and enthusiastic about Pokemon. The voice comes from that blend: someone with genuine knowledge and self-awareness navigating a world she recognizes from games, filtered through a personality that was always compatible with enthusiasm and wonder.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

Three modes alternate constantly:

**Short punch-ins** for emphasis, realization, emotional beats — often fragments:
> "A *Ditto*." (Ch. 2)

**Long winding sentences with embedded asides** — the analytical mind over-processing:
> "My Gyarados---level 40, carefully trained, survivor of multiple gym battles and approximately eight thousand random Pidgey and Mankey encounters---was down to just a sliver of health." (Ch. 1)

**Lists and rapid-fire enumeration** during overwhelm:
> "Not a sprite. Not a 3D model rotating on a screen. Not even the animated version from movies I'd watched a dozen times." (Ch. 2)

The signature is *variety* — frequent short punch-ins embedded between longer descriptive lines. Monotonous runs of similar length flatten the voice. Paragraphs are short (median 2 sentences), with single-sentence paragraphs doing specific work: impact beats, emotional turns, battle commands.

---

## Internal Monologue

Three mechanisms, often in the same chapter:

- **Single-quote direct thought** — rare, for the sharpest moments: "'Fuck,' I thought" (Ch. 1)
- **Italicized thought** — emotionally charged realizations: "*I touched a Ditto.*" (Ch. 2)
- **Narrative questioning** — the most frequent mode. Amber asks herself questions in narration: "Was I dead? Was I alive? I didn't know." (Ch. 1); "That made sense, right?" (Ch. 2)

---

## What the Adult Knowledge Adds

The adult analytical layer shows through **game knowledge** ("*Overheat*, I thought, my mind instinctively supplying the data. *A 130 base power special attack.*" — Ch. 17), **modern-world comparisons** ("the kind of place that promised clean sheets and zero questions" — Ch. 2), and **social pattern recognition** ("From their perspective, I must have looked like the world's most obvious distraction" — Ch. 8).

The body shows through **physical scale** ("I turned to the sink and went on tiptoes" — Ch. 2) and **sensory immediacy** — "A *real* Ditto, pink and gelatinous and impossibly, wonderfully *there*" (Ch. 2).

Early chapters tag physical sensations with wrongness ("My fingers found tangles in mint-green hair that still didn't feel like mine" — Ch. 2). This fades after Ch. 5 as the body becomes less alien.

---

## Humor

Amber's humor is a coping mechanism, not a worldview. She jokes about *her own* discomfort and absurdity, never about others' genuine suffering. She never jokes about Electabuzz's death, Fuji's violence, the original Amber's cancer, or Ash's genuine distress.

Key techniques: gaming metaphors applied to real life ("dealing with a crying child hadn't been part of any game guide I'd ever read" — Ch. 4), deadpan observation ("Nothing weird about that at all" — Ch. 2), incongruous register mixing ("Like a marshmallow attempting to intimidate" — Ch. 2), self-deprecation ("starring me, the world's least subtle intruder" — Ch. 8).

**The humor-to-gravity ratio shifts across the arc**: ~60-70% humor-inflected in Ch. 1-5, dropping to ~20-30% by Ch. 11-17. This is deliberate and should match story position.

**Comedy-to-gravity transitions are abrupt.** Don't signal the shift. The comedy stops and the weight arrives simultaneously. See the Ch. 10 ending (relief → slap → silence) and Ch. 14 (pancakes → door explosion).

---

## Pop-Culture / Isekai References

References thin as Amber accumulates direct experience: very dense in Ch. 1-5, moderate in Ch. 7-10, sparse by Ch. 11-17. When late references appear, they tend brief and more bitter: "Twenty years of anime episodes where he simply didn't exist, and here he was washing dishes." (Ch. 11)

Amber's reference pool is "college-age internet-literate gamer" — Pokemon games, anime, internet culture. No literary allusions, no high-culture references.

---

## Tonal Range

**Darkest register** (Ch. 15): sentences shorten, sensory detail becomes concrete, all humor drops, the game framework vanishes, similes disappear. Profanity appears only at high stakes.

**Brightest register** (Ch. 2-3): sentences lengthen, emphatic italics stack, sensory details pile up.

**Register shift markers**: sentence length contracts toward gravity; simile/metaphor disappear in the darkest passages; gaming lens vanishes; profanity signals high stakes.

---

## Distinctive Patterns

- **"Not X, but Y" construction**: bridges game expectations with reality. "Not a sprite. Not a 3D model... A *real* Ditto" (Ch. 2)
- **Kaomoji section breaks**: `\[@.@]/`, `\[^.^]/`, `-[?.?]-` etc. — unique to Amber's POV, never in serious/dark scenes, never in third-person chapters
- **Hedging**: "probably," "kind of," "maybe" are frequent. They thin in high-stakes moments.
- **Italics**: move names (*Overheat*), emphasis (*there*), emotionally charged thought (*I touched a Ditto.*)
