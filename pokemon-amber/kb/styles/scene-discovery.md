# Discovery / Exploration Scene Technique

Use this file when Amber encounters something new — a location, a Pokemon, a system. This is primarily Amber-specific (the dual-lens framework requires her game knowledge).

---

## The Dual-Lens Framework

Every discovery is filtered through two simultaneous frames: what Amber sees as reality, and what she remembers from games/anime. The gap between them is always the point:

> "Not a sprite. Not a 3D model rotating on a screen. Not even the animated version from movies I'd watched a dozen times. A *real* Ditto, pink and gelatinous and impossibly, wonderfully *there*." (Ch. 2)

The formula: state the game expectation → show how reality exceeds or subverts it → reality is consistently *more* in some dimension (scale, texture, moral weight, smell). The game version establishes the baseline; reality diverges; the divergence *is* the worldbuilding.

The signature sentence pattern is the **"Not X, but Y" construction**: "Not the simplified turn-based system I'd known from games" (Ch. 3); "No health bars floated above the Pokemon's heads" (Ch. 3).

---

## How the Prose Changes

**Sentences lengthen.** Discovery passages produce the longest sentences in the text as Amber takes in the world.

**Internal monologue hits maximum frequency.** Parenthetical commentary, meta-observations, game-knowledge cross-referencing. The voice is most distinctly *herself* when processing the world.

**Paragraphs grow** to 4-6 sentences (vs 1-3 in battle).

**Sensory inventory is systematic.** Each new location gets a deliberate pass — what it looks like, sounds like, smells like. Smell is notably privileged: "The hotel soap smelled aggressively floral---like someone had taken the concept of 'garden' and condensed it into a small bar." (Ch. 2)

---

## Worldbuilding Delivery

No pure exposition exists in the text. All worldbuilding routes through Amber's perspective and the dual-lens framework. Price tags ground the world in economic reality (vitamin prices in Ch. 9, hotel rates in Ch. 2). Genre conventions arrive with built-in context because Amber knows the source material — the worldbuilding is the *delta*.

---

## Calibration Over Time

The dual-lens comparisons thin as Amber accumulates direct experience: very dense in Ch. 1-5, moderate in Ch. 7-10, sparse by Ch. 11-17. The technique may need evolution as "first time" moments run out — shifting from "game vs reality" to "expected reality vs surprising reality."
