# Tension / Thriller Scene Technique

Use this file when writing scenes of dread, approach, or pre-violence — something terrible is coming and the prose knows it before the characters do. Distinct from battle (kinetic action) and emotional scenes (aftermath/feeling).

---

## Core Technique: Pacing Slows, Not Accelerates

Counter-intuitively, the tensest non-combat scenes use longer observations and more detail. The prose does not rush:

> "The doorbell chimed once. Nobody moved. It chimed again, followed by knocking. 'Delia?' Dr. Fuji's voice carried through the door, oddly calm. 'My love? I brought your favorites. Dahlias... just like our first date.'" (Ch. 14)

The horror is in the mundanity of the delivery — flowers, a nickname, a first-date memory.

---

## The Mundane-Horrific Contrast

The most consistent tension technique: the threatening element is presented in mundane, domestic, or professional terms. Flowers and pet names (Ch. 14). "A reasonable discussion" (Ch. 13). Protocol numbers (Ch. 13). "Unfortunate, but necessary" (Ch. 13). The disconnect between calm delivery and violent content is the mechanism.

---

## Sensory Detail Narrows

Where discovery scenes expand to take everything in, thriller scenes contract to one or two *wrong* details:

> "His white suit was immaculate, not a wrinkle or scorch mark. Pink and yellow petals scattered at his feet." (Ch. 14)

Two details: the suit (should be singed, isn't) and the petals. Both wrong in ways that communicate unraveling more efficiently than description.

---

## Sound Design

Sound events arrive before visual ones: "A car engine cut off. Footsteps crunched on gravel, slow and deliberate." (Ch. 14). Sound, then silence, then consequence. The silence between is where the dread lives.

---

## Four Forms of Tension in the Text

1. **Compression** (Ch. 10 ending) — comedy to violence with no transition
2. **Interior rationalization** (Ch. 13 Fuji POV) — locally coherent logic that's obviously broken
3. **Slow accumulation** (Ch. 14 approach/breach) — sound → silence → mundane horror → explosion
4. **Witnessing** (Ch. 15) — Amber present for violence she can't stop

The mundane-horrific contrast is currently specific to Fuji as the threat. Whether it transfers to other threat sources is an open question.
