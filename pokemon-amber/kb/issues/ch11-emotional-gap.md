# Ch. 11 Emotional Gap — Amber Passive During High-Stakes Content

**Scope**: Chapter-specific (Ch. 11)
**Severity**: Moderate-high — this is a pivotal scene that reads below its weight

---

## The Problem

Ch. 11 contains the Pallet Town reveal: Delia rejecting the clone, Fuji's breakdown, Amber witnessing her own rejection. This should be one of the most emotionally devastating moments in the story.

Instead, Amber is largely passive. The prose gives us observation, not feeling. We see what happens but don't experience Amber's interior response at the depth the moment demands.

---

## Evidence

The chapter opening handles objects-as-absence well:
> "Cold tea in a forgotten mug. An empty chair where Dr. Fuji should be. And there, the empty space by the kettle where Ditto's Poké Ball used to sit, a glaring absence."

But during the confrontation between Fuji and Delia — the moment that most directly affects Amber's identity and future — the prose stays observational. Amber watches. She reports. She doesn't break.

Compare to Ch. 2 (bathroom scene) where physical crisis is rendered in stopped fragments, body facts, no commentary. Or Ch. 15 (Electabuzz death) where dissociative calm is rendered precisely. Ch. 11 gets neither technique.

---

## Possible Diagnosis

1. **Intentional shock rendering**: Amber may be too overwhelmed to feel, and the observational distance is the shock. If so, the shock needs physical rendering — numbness is a body state, not an absence of prose.

2. **Narration defaulting to safe mode**: The analytical/observational default is easier to write than the full emotional register. The prose may have stayed in commentary mode because the emotional technique requires committing to it.

3. **Scene construction**: Amber is a spectator to the Fuji-Delia confrontation. She's not a participant until later. This structural positioning may have pulled the prose toward observation.

---

## What the Scene Needs (Not Prescriptive — Questions)

- When Delia says "That's not---that's not Amber," what happens to Amber's body?
- When Fuji says "I brought our daughter back," does Amber's adult mind react to being called a product?
- Is there a moment where the game-knowledge framework tries to fire and fails — where there's no reference point for being rejected as a person?
- Does the child body respond even when the adult mind can't process?

---

## Chapters Affected

Primarily Ch. 11. Similar but less severe distance appears in Ch. 17 aftermath scenes.
