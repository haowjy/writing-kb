# Dual-Lens Discovery Mode — Shelf Life Question

**Scope**: Structural/voice concern for future chapters
**Severity**: Low (not yet a problem, but approaching)

---

## The Concern

The dual-lens framework (game expectation vs reality) is the most distinctive and consistent technique in the text. Every discovery scene uses it. Through Ch. 17, the comparisons are varied enough that it hasn't calcified.

But Amber is accumulating direct experience. The game-to-reality comparisons are already thinning naturally (dense in Ch. 1-5, moderate in Ch. 7-10, sparse in Ch. 11-17). As she continues to live in this world, the "first time" moments will run out.

---

## The Question

When Amber has experienced enough of this world that she no longer reflexively compares to the games, what does the discovery voice become?

**Options** (not prescriptive):
1. **Shift from "game vs reality" to "expected reality vs surprising reality"** — Amber now has in-world expectations that can be subverted
2. **The comparisons become nostalgic rather than calibrating** — she reaches for a game reference and catches herself, feeling the distance from her old life
3. **The technique thins to zero and discovery scenes use a different framework** — sensory inventory without the dual lens
4. **New "first times" in different domains** — she's experienced Celadon and Pallet Town, but not other regions, legendaries, the Pokemon League system, etc.

---

## Current State

Not yet a problem. The natural thinning is working. But the next arc should plan for how discovery scenes work when the game framework is no longer the primary lens.

---

## Evidence of Natural Evolution Already Happening

> "Twenty years of anime episodes where he simply didn't exist, and here he was washing dishes." (Ch. 11)

This isn't the "game vs reality" comparison — it's more personal and bitter. The reference serves characterization (what Amber clings to) rather than worldbuilding (what's different here). This may be the natural evolution path.
