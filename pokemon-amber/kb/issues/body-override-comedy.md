# Body-Override Comedy

## Pattern
Scenes where the child body's neurochemistry "hijacks" the adult mind for comedy — the sugar rush reaction in Ch. 14, the caffeine meltdown in Ch. 9.

## Problem
These scenes lean on a gimmick ("watch the adult brain get overridden by child biology") that doesn't serve the character or story. The joke is the same both times: adult loses control of child body's reactions, comedy ensues. It reinforces an adult/child binary that doesn't reflect what's actually happening with Amber's character — her personality was already compatible with childhood; the body isn't "overriding" an otherwise composed adult.

## Scope
Ch. 9 (caffeine), Ch. 14 (sugar). May recur if writers treat body-override as an established comedy technique.

## Severity
Low — these are contained scenes, not structural. But they set a pattern that could become a crutch.

## Evidence
- Ch. 9: caffeine rant — "I'm fine! Totally fine. Super fine. Never been better. Did you know your hair looks like spaghetti when it moves?" — the comedy comes from chemical override, not character
- Ch. 14: sugar hit — "the sweetness hit my taste buds like a revelation. My adult mind registered it as overwhelmingly saccharine, but my body responded with pure delight" — explicitly frames it as adult-vs-child-body
